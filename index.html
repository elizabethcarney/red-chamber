By Elizabeth Carney

A map of Prospect Garden painted by 孙温 (Sun Wen) in the late 19th century. The white wall around the Garden is clearly visible, penning in its fantastical mansions and landscaping.


A Boundary between Worlds


The Garden’s main gate represents the boundary between real life and illusory paradise. It is a handsome gatehouse, sparingly decorated with wood and marble carvings, and on its either side you see the bare whitewashed wall surrounding the Garden. The side of the gate facing the outside world features “exquisite workmanship” but is “unadorned by colour or gilding.” It only has the marble carvings on the floor and a “simple pattern” at its bottom for decoration (327). To a visitor, the gate would look stately and well-built, but plain. Its simple features model Confucian values such as order and elegant functionality.

A painting of Jia Yuanchun, the Imperial Concubine and Baoyu’s treasured older sister. Painted by An Ho, 1993, watercolor on silk mounted on rice paper using traditional Chinese technique. She is formally depicted in an imperial gown and crown, highlighting her stature.


Yet, as soon as you cross the threshold, you are drawn into a world of fantasy and beauty; through the doors you see a dramatic mountain covered in lush foliage (327). The rest of the vast park is even more impressive. Shimmering lakes, fields of flowers, artful pavilions, and grand mansions are everywhere you look. The Garden is truly a paradise full of luxury, and it functions as a stark contrast to the outside world. There is an abundance of passion, youth, beauty, and opulence within the Garden walls. The main gate is a clear symbol of the Garden’s other-worldliness, and it asserts that the dreams found inside could never survive in the real world.


The Origin of the Garden


The Garden is created so that the Imperial Concubine Jia Yuanchun, Baoyu’s much-loved older cousin, can visit home. The Palace requires that she must stay in a secure place, and since she is royalty, it must be luxurious as well. The Jia family spares no expense to construct the most extravagant haven they can for her. After Yuanchun’s visit, she suggests that the girls of the household move into the various dwellings in the Garden to enjoy it to the fullest, and Baoyu is allowed to join them.


The girls’ arrival breathes life into the Garden again. Baoyu is “blissfully happy”; he spends all of his time surrounded by his cousins and maids, playing instruments, writing poetry, singing, reading, writing calligraphy, and soaking in the natural beauty surrounding him (460). Living in the Garden is his paradise. He later refers to his early days in the Garden as the “golden ages,” and remembers being surrounded by the girls with great nostalgia.
